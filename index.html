Volcanoes: A Blessing and a Curse

By Gian Cella



A volcano is defined as a conical landform or mountain containing one or more vents fed by magma rising directly from great depths within the earth. They can be a destructive force that brings forth death and despair while also bringing about renewal and prosperity. Volcanoes have formed the continents that humans thrive on today as well as outgassed water from the mantle to create the atmosphere; they also produce fertile soil and mineral deposits in their wake in addition to geothermal energy. Nevertheless, these structures have killed thousands of people and even destroyed entire cities and ecosystems.

There are approximately 1,500 potentially active volcanoes on land today and even more that are lying dormant. Counting the ones on the ocean floor, the number of volcanoes on planet Earth increase to tens of thousands. Geologists have grouped these various volcanoes into four main kinds:

  1. Cinder Cones
  2. Lava Domes
  3. Composite Volcano
  4. Shield Volcano

Each volcano is comprised of unique characteristics consisting of:

  • Magma Composition
  • Viscosity
  • Explosiveness
  • Gas Content

Cinder cones are the simplest volcanoes and are built from pieces of solidified lava released from only one vent; the lava breaks into small fragments that congeal and fall as cinders around the vent to form an ovular cone. Additionally, they often occur in groups, have steep slope angle, and are rather small. On the other hand, shield volcanoes are built almost entirely of lava flows that pour out in all directions from one or more vents and pile up thousands of lava flows that cool in order to become thin, flat sheets. This accretion of sheets resemble that of a warrior's shield, resulting in some of the largest volcanoes in the world.

Similarly, lava domes are formed by spherical bodies of lava too thick to flow great distances which end up piling over and around its vent upon extrusion. These volcanic structures occur mainly in the craters or flanks of composite volcanoes. In regards to composite volcanoes, they are some of the most violent volcanoes and are known as stratovolcanoes. They are usually steep, large, symmetrical cones built of fluctuating layers of lava flows, volcanic ash, cinders, blocks, bombs, and any other volcanic projectiles. Most composite volcanoes have a crater at its zenith which contains one or more vents, but what these structures are most known for are their conduit system through which magma from a reservoir deep in the Earth's crust rises to the surface. With all their similarities, volcanoes are grouped by geologists based on many factors that discern them from one another. As mentioned previously, these factors are magma composition, viscosity, explosiveness, and gas content. Magma composition refers to the types and abundances of constituent minerals that can determine where the magma was formed. Basaltic and gabbroic magmas, for example, have a SiO2 content of fifty percent and become molten at 1,200 degrees Celsius; they contain high levels of magnesium, iron, and calcium but low quantities of potassium and sodium. Andesitic and dioritic magmas have a SiO2 content of sixty percent and become molten at 1,000 degrees Celsius. They are comprised of medium amounts of magnesium, iron, and calcium but low potassium and sodium. Rhyolitic and granitic magmas have seventy percent SiO2 content as well as a melting point of 900 degrees Celsius. They consist of low magnesium, iron, and calcium distribution but high sodium and potassium. With this information in mind, these magmas form igneous rock, or solid lava/magma, when they cool down; there are two kinds of igneous rocks, intrusive and extrusive, that differ in grain size and cooling speed.

In regards to viscosity and gas content, they both play a role in how destructive a volcanic eruption can be. Viscosity is defined as a materialâ€™s resistance to flow, or thickness in other words, and gas content refers to how much gas, usually carbon dioxide and water vapor, magma contains. Viscosity is increased with less heat, more silica, and more escaped gas. High viscosity along with high gas content creates the volcanic eruptions people hear about in history books with thick, sluggish lava flows and explosive activity that releases huge sulfur clouds with hot debris particles called pyroclasts. Low viscosity along with low gas content is the opposite; the lava flows are quick and fluid because the trapped gas is not released which results in no gas explosion.
